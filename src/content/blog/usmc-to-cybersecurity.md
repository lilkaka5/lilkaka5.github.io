I enlisted in the Marine Corps in 2008. I was 18, knew nothing about IT, and had no plan beyond serving. Four years later I separated with an honorable discharge and started figuring out what came next.

What I didn't realize until years into my security career is that the Corps had already taught me most of what matters in this field — just not in the way I expected.

## The OODA Loop Is a Security Framework

Marines internalize Boyd's OODA loop — **Observe, Orient, Decide, Act** — long before they know what it's called. You're constantly processing information, updating your situational picture, making decisions with incomplete data, and moving.

In incident response, this is exactly how you work. An alert fires. You observe: what's the signal, what's the noise? You orient: what does this pattern mean given what I know about this environment? You decide: escalate, contain, investigate further? You act — and then the loop starts over.

The operators who freeze in an incident are the ones who wait for certainty before acting. Certainty doesn't come. You work with what you have and you move.

## Risk Is Never Zero — Learn to Accept the Residual

The Marine Corps doesn't train you to eliminate risk. That's impossible. It trains you to understand the mission, assess the threats, mitigate what you can, and accept what you can't — then execute anyway.

This maps almost perfectly to how mature security organizations think about risk. The job isn't to say "no" to everything. The job is to understand the risk appetite of the organization, identify the controls that bring exposure to an acceptable level, and communicate clearly when residual risk remains.

I've seen security teams that were so risk-averse they became obstacles rather than enablers. The Corps taught me that risk paralysis is its own kind of failure.

## Small Unit Leadership = Security Team Leadership

I was a lance corporal responsible for logistics support in a small unit. Leadership responsibility at that level is intensely direct — you know your people, you're accountable for their performance, and there's no bureaucracy to hide behind.

Running a security team feels similar. Whether it's a three-person analyst team or a larger organization, the fundamentals don't change: clear mission, clear standards, take care of your people, and hold the line when it counts.

The best leaders I've had in this industry shared qualities with the best NCOs I served with: direct communication, high standards, and genuine investment in the people around them.

## The Transition Isn't Easy — But the Skills Transfer

Getting out of the military and into a technical career is genuinely hard. The certifications, the vocabulary, the culture — it's a lot to navigate simultaneously. I leaned heavily on structured learning (CompTIA, then eventually CISSP) to build a credential foundation while the experience caught up.

If you're a veteran looking at cybersecurity: your instincts about discipline, mission focus, and operating under pressure are real advantages. Don't undersell them. The technical skills are learnable. The mindset you already have.

The Corps made me a better security professional than I knew at the time. That's probably true for a lot of veterans in this field.
